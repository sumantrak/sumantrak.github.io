Smarter Decisions with Multi-Armed Bandits

Created on May 25, 2025

2025   ·   bandits   machine-learning   online-learning   ·   ml-theory

🎰 Learning by Trying: Why Bandits Matter

Picture this.

You’re running a clinical trial for a new drug. Patients are coming in daily. You have multiple treatments to choose from, but no clear winner yet. Every choice you make affects a real person — and every wrong choice is a missed opportunity to help. You don’t want to guess. You want to learn as you go, adapt based on feedback, and improve decisions over time.

This isn’t just medicine. The same challenge shows up in personalized ads, recommendation systems, autonomous agents, A/B testing, and even education platforms. In all these domains, we face a common dilemma:

How do we make the best possible decisions while still learning what the best is?

This is the heart of the Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) problem — a deceptively simple framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty.

Let’s start at the beginning — with a gambler, a handful of slot machines, and a question that’s deceptively simple:
Which one should I pull next?

Multi-Armed Bandits (MABs) offer a powerful way to make sequential decisions under uncertainty, where each action provides feedback that can improve future choices. From online advertising to clinical trials, this framework appears in some of the most high-impact applications of AI.

🎰 Why Are They Called “Bandits”?

The term “multi-armed bandit” comes from the classic slot machine analogy — also known as a one-armed bandit. Each machine (or arm) offers an unknown probability of payout. You can pull one arm at a time and want to win as much as possible over time.

This metaphor was formalized in the 1950s by statistician Herbert Robbins, whose foundational work focused on adaptive allocation and regret minimization. The core challenge? Balancing exploration (trying new arms) with exploitation (using the best-known option so far).

🔁 Sequential Decision-Making: The Core Setting

MABs represent a minimal model of online learning where:

  • You must repeatedly choose between multiple actions.
  • Each action gives limited feedback.
  • There’s no full supervision, only rewards from chosen actions.

This structure is ideal when data is not fully labeled or feedback is expensive.

🎯 What Are We Optimizing For?

1. Cumulative Regret

The most common objective: maximize total reward over time, or equivalently, minimize cumulative regret.

\[ R_T = T \cdot \mu^* - \sum_{t=1}^T \mu_{a_t} \]

Where \( \mu^* \) is the true mean reward of the best arm, and \( a_t \) is the arm pulled at time \( t \).

Used in:

  • Online advertising
  • A/B testing
  • Recommendation systems

2. Simple Regret

The goal here is to identify the best arm, not necessarily to accumulate reward during learning.

This is key when:

  • Only the final decision matters (e.g., best drug).
  • The cost of exploration is acceptable, but accuracy is critical.

3. PAC (Probably Approximately Correct)

We want to identify an arm that’s within ( \epsilon ) of optimal with high probability ( 1 - \delta ). This blends sample efficiency with statistical guarantees.

4. Risk-Sensitive Objectives

In high-stakes environments, it’s not enough to optimize the mean. Instead, we focus on:

  • Variance-aware strategies
  • Minimax regret
  • CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk)

Applications: finance, autonomous systems, healthcare.

📊 What Kind of Feedback Can We Get?

The classic setting assumes stochastic numeric rewards, but real-world feedback is richer and messier.

✅ Stochastic Numerical

  • Each arm gives a random reward from a fixed distribution.
  • Algorithms: UCB, Thompson Sampling

✅ Binary Feedback

  • Especially used in click-through data, where reward ∈ {0, 1}.
  • Simple but powerful in large-scale recommender systems.

✅ Ordinal / Preference-Based

  • Feedback is comparative: “Arm A > Arm B”.
  • Useful in:
    • Human preferences
    • Ranking problems
    • Crowdsourcing

A popular framework: Dueling Bandits

✅ Adversarial Feedback

  • No assumptions about reward generation.
  • The worst-case scenario is considered.
  • Algorithms: EXP3, Hedge

Used in:

  • Network routing
  • Cybersecurity
  • Competitive markets

✅ Contextual Feedback

  • Each decision comes with a context vector (e.g., user profile).
  • The best arm depends on context.

Applications:

  • Personalized recommendations
  • Adaptive user interfaces
